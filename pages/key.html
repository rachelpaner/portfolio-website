Key Design Studio Internship

Project Type: Mobile Application, Client Project
Timeline: June 2023-August 2023
Role: UX Researcher, Design Strategist
Team: 3 designers

Background

I spent the summer as an intern at the Key Design Studio, which is an embedded change agency that aims to transform the historical KeyBank through design thinking. Part of this mission includes developing and deploying a design system to maximize consistency and efficiency across the company's 20+ different product teams, which has reached barriers related to resource allocation and convincing upper-level stakeholders of its business value.

Together, the 2 other studio interns and myself took ownership of a capstone project to dive deeper into these issues and propose recommendations to scale the design system across the enterprise.

The Challenge

Product development teams across the company inconsistently adopted the design system because of a lack of compatibility with the products' different user needs and existing frameworks and an unclear value propostition for adoption.

Furthermore, the current design system team consisted of 10 designers and just 2 developers working on a voluntary basis outside of their higher-priority projects, leading to resource and time constraints that dampened cross-collaboration, regular system updates, and organization of materials.

These issues in and outside of the studio resulted in unproductive internal workflows and an incoherent design vision within the the company.

Timeline

Research

  1. Current State Product Landscape

    2. We surveyed stakeholders for over 20 different products to get a better idea of how the design system is currently used. We found that almost half of the products did not reference the design system at all, reflecting a significant inconsistency across workflows and user experience.

  2. Stakeholder Interviews

    3. In order to expand on this snapshot of the product landscape and gain a holistic view of the varying perspectives on the design system, we interviewed 20 different stakeholders across the enterprise, including in-studio designers, developers, product owners, marketing, and several industry competitors, such as Capital One and Intuit.

  3. Interview Insights
    1. Stakeholders don’t see the value of a design system

      Outside of the studio, many stakeholders view the design system as an unnecessary change to their workflow, and the extra time, manpower, and financial resources needed to implement the system is not adequately justified by the perceived benefits.

    2. Communication and consistency is difficult across a large enterprise

      Each product team has different needs and processes that are largely unknown to those outside of a single team. Factors such as varying product timelines, internal competition, and distinct lines of business causes inconsistent workflow and user experience across products and prevents a steady cadence between different stakeholder groups.

    3. There is no attachment to clear business goals and design values.

      The design system's lack of a clear vision affects its perceived business value. The design system has resulted in more efficient prioritization, and more consistent user experience across products, but these successes have not been effectively framed in a digestible, organized way that resonates across the entire enterprise.

  4. Competitive Analysis

    5. After establishing our pain points, we compiled 9 different industry design systems, including Shopify, IBM, and AirBnB, to learn more about best practices related to subjects such as governance, collaboration, documentation, and code frameworks for large-scale adoption.

    Salesforce’s governance system involves collaboration between centralized, federated, and solitary teams, creating a cyclical model that enables constant research, testing, and updating of the design system.
    IBM holds design jams, where stakeholders across product teams cross-collaborate on singular areas of inconsistency in the design system.
    Apple's Human Interface Guidelines are constantly updated and provide comprehensive information on every aspect of the system, such as inclusive communication and spatial layout.
    Microsoft's FAST uses web components to build adaptive UI that is compatible with almost any modern web framework
  5. Secondary Research

    6. Our interview and competitive analysis insights helped guide our secondary research, which revolved around:

    1. Encouraging cross-collaboration to establish a foundational design vision across products that can be used to increase visibility on the value of a design system.
    2. Creating formal governance processes and comprehensive documentation to maintain transparency and streamline internal management, updates, and cross-platform communication.
    3. Aligning design to code and removing implementation barriers across different frameworks through web components, APIs, and Figma plugins.

how might we...
promote the value of the design system across the enterprise in order to increase resource allocation, adoption and cross-disciplinary collaboration?
establish formal governing processes to streamline management of the design system and meet the needs of different products across the enterprise?
update code frameworks and leverage new tools for component creation to enable developers to efficiently implement the design system enterprise-wide?

Ideation and Prototyping

Using our How Might We statements as a guide, we then organized our ideas using an Importance-Difficulty matrix to evaluate our solutions based on constraints and feasability. The matrix helped prioritize and frame our solutions into a Now, Next, Later format that we employed for our final roadmap.

Throughout the prototyping process, we iterated based on additional feedback from stakeholders and industry experts that identified potential concerns and points for expansion. For example, we met with enterprise Figma consultants to get an in-depth review of the new Figma tools, such as variables and modes, and validated that this could be a realistic solution to building reusable components that could satisfy multiple different product needs.

Our final deliverable includes 25 recommendations broken down into our three themes of governance, collaboration, and code, and each recommendation corresponds to an appendix page with supporting research and explanation for further reference and guidance. The roadmap establishes a general timeline that leaves room for exploration and adaptation, but provides enough detail to stakeholders both in and outside of the studio to understand the purpose and actionability of the current recommendations.

Testing and Outcomes

Our process culminated in a presentation to our studio stakeholders, paired with an Assumption-Reversal workshop. Through this exercise, we were able to get the design team to directly apply the information that they had learned to their own experiences with the design system, as well as simulate a vision alignment workshop on a smaller scale before expanding it to non-design stakeholders.

Overall, our roadmap gained an overwhelmingly positive response from the studio, with team members expressing both that they resonated with our points, and learned about new pain points and constraints from non-design perspective that they had not previously considered. Aligning with our "Now" recommendations, the roadmap itself will be a critical tool in future conversations with leadership to increase top-down buy-in, advocating for the design system's value in a company that is historically rooted in legacy systems and bottom lines.

What I Learned

Design strategy in practice

This project helped me understand the applications of end-to-end human-centered design process outside of a singular product context, and how to utilitze research and design thinking tools to navigate abstract deliverables

The importance of empathy within enterprise

We interviewed a diverse set of stakeholders with varying perspectives of the design system, both positive and negative. Thus, it was extremely important for us to establish a tone of psychological safety with our interviewees and express our that we were there to learn from them, not push the design system onto them.

Leverage all available resources

With so many research initiatives and stakeholders, we took every opportunity to reach out to colleagues for points of contact for interviews, access to reosurces such as Forrester Reports, expert references such as the Figma team, and design thinking tools to organize and synthesize our findings.
